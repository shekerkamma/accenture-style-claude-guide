# Commercial Structuring

## When To Use

Use when the commercial section of a proposal must be written, or when a client is challenging the proposed fee structure. Commercial structuring is distinct from pricing — it defines the model (how fees are structured and when risk is shared), not just the number.

## Consulting Approach

Accenture uses five commercial models: Time-and-Materials (T&M), Fixed Price (FP), Managed Service, Outcome-Based (fees tied to measurable results), and Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT). The right model depends on scope certainty, client risk appetite, engagement type, and the firm's confidence in delivery. Outcome-based models win competitive bids but require a defensible measurement framework before signing.

## Workflow

1. Assess scope certainty: how well-defined is the work? High certainty → Fixed Price viable. Low certainty → T&M or phase-by-phase approach.
2. Assess client risk appetite: does the client want to share risk or transfer it? Risk transfer to the firm requires a premium or outcome upside.
3. Select the commercial model and structure the fee.
4. Build the resource plan and cost model underpinning the fee.
5. Calculate the margin at the proposed fee and at a 10–15% reduction (the likely negotiation range).
6. Define the assumptions and exclusions that protect the firm if scope expands.

## Quality Bar

- Margin must be calculated at two scenarios below the proposed fee — know the floor before the negotiation begins.
- Assumptions must be specific enough that a scope dispute can be adjudicated against them.
- Outcome-based models must define the measurement methodology before the proposal is submitted — "to be agreed" measurement is a liability.
- The resource plan must be achievable with the actual team available, not a theoretical optimal team.
